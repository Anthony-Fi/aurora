How to Photograph the Northern Lights

Practical tips from an experienced aurora chaser

Read guide

This guide is for anyone who wants to take high-quality photographs of the Northern Lights—without getting lost in jargon or gear obsession. I've spent years chasing auroras, forgetting memory cards (twice), and learning what works through trial, error, and a few cold nights. Here's what I've learned.

1. Before You Go: Planning & Preparation

Northern Lights photography is easy when you know how. But it starts with knowing when and where to look.

  • Track the Storms: Use aurora forecast sites and apps to monitor KP index, solar wind speed, and density. These numbers help you know when a storm is coming and how strong it might be.
  • Scout Locations in Daylight: Look for places with a clear view north and interesting foreground features—trees, lakes, cabins, anything that adds scale.
  • Check Your Gear: I've forgotten memory cards twice. Now I keep a spare in the car. Always double-check your kit before heading out.
  • Avoid Light Pollution: DarkSiteFinder.com is a great tool to find dark sky locations.

2. Gear That Works (From My Own Bag)

You don't need the latest gear, but you do need the right kind. Here's what I use and recommend:

  • Canon 6D + EF 24mm f/2.8 – Wide shots, handles high ISO well.
  • Canon 7D + Tamron 24–70mm – Cropped shots, best kept at ISO 800 to avoid noise.
  • Sony A7s (pre-owned) – Incredible for low light and even live streaming auroras.

Essentials:

  • Tripod (a must for long exposures)
  • Intervalometer or timer (for star trails and time lapses)
  • Wide-angle lens (14–35mm range)
  • Spare batteries and memory cards
  • LED torches (for lighting foreground objects)
  • Sealable bags (wildlife safety—more on that later)

3. Camera Settings: Start Simple

Here's a basic manual setup that works well:

Aperture: f/2.8–f/4
ISO: 800
Shutter Speed: 10 seconds
Focus: Manual, set to infinity

Notes from the field:

  • Aperture: Some lenses perform better slightly stopped down. Try f/2.8, then f/3.2 or f/4. Check the edges for vignetting.
  • Exposure Time: I usually start at 4 seconds and go up to 12 depending on ISO. Too long and you'll blur the stars.
  • The 500 Rule: For full-frame cameras, divide 500 by your lens focal length to keep stars sharp. Use 600 for crop sensors. PhotoPills has a great calculator.
  • ISO: Start at 800. Higher ISO brightens the image but adds noise. Newer cameras like the Sony A7sII and A7sIII handle high ISO beautifully.

4. Composition Tips: Make It Yours

Foreground Matters: A good foreground gives scale and context. Trees, rocks, buildings—anything that anchors the sky.

Star Trails: If the aurora is mild, try creating star trails with an intervalometer.

Selfies with Aurora: Use a torch to light yourself gently. You can even use colored film or gels for creative lighting effects.

5. Night Noises & Wildlife

This part's not in most guides—but it should be.

I've heard elk thudding around, heavy breathing in the dark, foxes screaming, and owls hooting. It's wild out there. In Finland, there are bears. They usually avoid people, but if you're in remote areas (which aurora photographers often are), make noise as you move. Take your rubbish with you and seal food in bags.

6. Practice Makes Progress

Taking great aurora photos takes time. And yes, it's tempting to keep buying new gear—but don't get lost in that rabbit hole.

  • Look at other photographers' aurora shots and see what gear they used.
  • Buy pre-owned when you can. It saves money and still delivers.
  • Check your images often. Adjust focus, ISO, and exposure until it feels right.

Final Thoughts

All of the above—and a good foreground—will help you take amazing photographs of the Northern Lights. It's not just about the gear or the settings. It's about being out there, under the sky, listening to the night, and capturing something that feels like magic.
